# Founder interview

Use these questions at the start of **build mode**. Batch them into one message so the founder can answer asynchronously — don't ping them with one question at a time.

Organize the output in a way they can fill in. Warn them that thin answers produce a thin pitch — 10 minutes of honest answers here saves hours of rewrites later.

---

## How to ask

Start the interview message with something like:

> To build a deck that actually lands, I need the raw material. Answer these as honestly as you can — short bullets are fine, but don't skip. If you don't know an answer (e.g., growth rate, competitor analysis), say "don't know" and I'll flag it as a gap to close before submission. I'll ask follow-ups only if I need them.

Then send the questions grouped by section. Group headers help the founder see the structure of what's coming.

---

## Section 1 — Product essentials

1. **What do you call the product?** (name + one-sentence what-it-does, in your own voice — not marketing-speak)
2. **Who's it for?** (one user archetype, concrete — "crypto-earning freelancers in SE Asia" beats "Web3 users")
3. **In one sentence, what can the user *do* with it that they can't do today?** (this becomes the value proposition)
4. **What's the one feature you want to show in the demo?** (20–30 sec of screen time — pick the flow that makes a non-user go "oh, I get it")
5. **Is there a Web2 product I could say "X for Y" about your product?** (e.g., "Kickstarter for crypto products", "Pokémon Go for DeFi") — optional but useful

## Section 2 — The problem

6. **What's the one main problem you solve?** (not three — pick the biggest)
7. **What happens to users if this problem stays unsolved?** (lost money / lost time / missed opportunity — which?)
8. **Do you have a stat, news event, or tweet that proves the problem is real and urgent?** (paste it — this goes on the problem slide)
9. **What did *you* discover about the problem that other founders in the space haven't noticed?** (this is your founder insight — the thing that makes the problem feel new)
10. **Is the problem obvious to a crypto-native judge, or does it need education?** (e.g., ICO dumps = obvious; SE Asia local QR-code payment limits = needs education)

## Section 3 — Market + Business

11. **Who is the target user category and roughly how big is it?** (prefer real user categories and counts over TAM/SAM/SOM)
12. **Is there a trend showing this market is growing?** (1–3 growth % with sources, if you have them)
13. **Who's the most obvious competitor a judge would ask about?** (name 1–3 max; if none, say "none")
14. **Where are you better than them, in one dimension?** (don't try to win on all dimensions — pick one)
15. **How do you make money?** (transaction fee %, subscription, marketplace take rate, spread — one line)

## Section 4 — Traction (be honest)

16. **Revenue to date?** (any amount — $50 of real revenue beats 10k waitlist signups)
17. **Real product usage numbers?** (active users, transactions, matches played, GMV — whatever is your core metric)
18. **Growth rate week-over-week or month-over-month?** (if you've been live ≥3 weeks)
19. **Anything notable vs industry average?** (e.g., "10× the retention of comparable mobile games" — extrapolation is allowed)
20. **Partnerships, grants, accelerators, advisors?** (with one line on what each one *does* for you, not just logos)
21. **⚠️ Do not include X/Twitter followers.** Judges read this as a red flag. Skip it.

## Section 5 — Growth + Roadmap

22. **Target for the next 6 months?** (e.g., 10k users, $100k MRR, launch on new chain)
23. **Three concrete distribution channels to get there?** (not 6 vague ones — 3 solid ones)
24. **One big thing shipping next quarter?** (partnership, feature, geography, platform launch — pick one)
25. **One big thing shipping in 6 months?**
26. **One big thing shipping in 9–12 months?**

## Section 6 — Team

27. **Who's on the team, and what's the *one outstanding achievement* the team has already done that's relevant to this product?** (e.g., "we built a mobile game that reached 320k users" — not "we have 10 years of experience")
28. **Any notable advisors or mentors?** (especially for solo founders — named advisors at the top of your field is gold)

## Section 7 — Context + Constraints

29. **Are you submitting to Colosseum Frontier, another hackathon, a demo day, or an investor pitch?** (affects specifics)
30. **Pitch length limit?** (default is 3 minutes for Colosseum; let me know if different)
31. **Is this for a recorded video, live stage, or both?**
32. **What's the call-to-action you want at the end?** (longer demo video, book a call, try the product, scan QR to Legends.fund, etc.)
33. **Deadline?** (so I can scope how much polish is realistic — a 3-day turnaround looks different from a 3-week turnaround)
34. **Any constraint I should know?** (team is solo, no designer, no video experience, limited time for rehearsal, etc.)

---

## Follow-up protocol

Don't ask every follow-up. If an answer is thin, ask at most one follow-up question, and only for the sections that matter most: **problem (Q6-10), traction (Q16-20), team (Q27-28)**. These are where weak material becomes visible to a judge.

For the rest (market, business model, growth), you can often write a reasonable draft from a thin answer and let the founder correct in review.

---

## Gaps to flag

After reading the founder's answers, flag gaps **before drafting**. Common ones:

- No data on the problem → draft will feel like intuition, not research. Ask them to spend 30 min finding one stat.
- "Don't know" on growth rate → draft will have to skip the best traction framing. Ask if they can pull last 3 weeks of user counts.
- Team slide material is all bios, no achievement → push back: "what's the outstanding thing *this* team has done?"
- No CTA → push back: "what do you want the judge to *do* at 2:55?"

Flagging gaps up-front is kinder than shipping a draft that has to be rewritten.
