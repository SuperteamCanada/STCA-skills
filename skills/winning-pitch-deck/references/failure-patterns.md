# The 5 failure patterns

After coaching 200+ founders, Sofian identified five patterns that kill pitches. Before delivering any draft or review, run through this self-check. If the pitch fails on any of these, fix before showing the user.

---

## 1. No strong hook

**The failure:** the first 15 seconds are generic ("Hi, we're X, we do Y in the blockchain space") and the judge's attention is already gone. The rest of the pitch plays to an empty room.

**How to check:** read (or watch) only the first 15 seconds. Would a tired VC watching their 47th pitch of the day keep watching?

**Fixes:**
- Open with a surprising stat or news event from the problem space
- Open with authority if the team has exceptional traction or credentials (put traction before the problem)
- Open with a provocative framing of the problem ("67% of Kickstarter backers never come back — and it's not their fault")
- Open with a vivid scene ("I was in Bangkok trying to pay for dinner with crypto and…")

**Do not open with:**
- "Hi, my name is… and today I'd like to talk about…"
- "In the rapidly growing world of [space]…"
- Visionary mission-speak.

---

## 2. No trust (future hopes instead of concrete facts)

**The failure:** the pitch talks about what the team *hopes* to build, *aims* to do, *is trying* to solve. Nothing concrete has shipped, no data backs the claims, the tense is soft. The judge concludes "this is a concept, not a company".

**How to check:**
- Grep the script for: *trying*, *hope*, *aim*, *would*, *should*, *could*, *we will*, *we plan to*, *in the future*. Every instance outside the roadmap slide is a trust leak.
- Check every stat for a source. "Millions of users struggle with…" without a source is a trust leak.
- Check traction: is it real numbers, or is it "we're expecting to reach…"?

**Fixes:**
- Rewrite every conditional verb into present or past tense
- Back every claim with a number + source
- Put the business model slide in (if missing)
- Move traction higher if it's strong

---

## 3. Problem not sold (skipped or rushed)

**The failure:** the founder is so excited about the solution that they breeze through the problem in 10 seconds, and the judge never *feels* the pain. "If you sell the problem, you don't need to sell the solution" — inverse: if you skip the problem, the solution has nothing to stand on.

**How to check:**
- Does the problem slide have *data* backing it?
- Does the problem slide name a consequence (lost money, lost time, missed opportunity)?
- Does the script spend ≥25 seconds on the problem in a 3-min pitch?
- Does a judge who doesn't know the space feel the pain after watching the problem slide?

**Fixes:**
- Add a concrete stat
- Add a user story or news event
- Add an image that makes the pain visceral
- If the space is crowded, add founder-insight narrative ("we studied this, tried X, realized Y…")
- If the space is niche, add a relatable comparison ("this is Kickstarter's problem, but for crypto")

---

## 4. Cluttered or draft-quality slides

**The failure:** slides have 10 bullets, tiny text, no brand identity, default Google Slides fonts. The judge gives up trying to read and tunes out. Or the opposite: slides so text-heavy the judge reads ahead and stops listening to the founder.

**How to check:**
- Any slide with more than ~15 words? Flag it.
- Any slide title longer than 4 words? Flag it.
- Is there a consistent brand identity (color palette, font, logo usage)?
- Would a judge understand the slide's message in <2 seconds of scanning?

**Fixes:**
- Split any crowded slide into 2–3 slides (one slide = one message)
- Cut title to 3–4 words
- Invest an hour in a simple brand identity: two fonts, three colors, logo consistency
- Use animations to reveal list items one at a time
- Add an image or icon per slide to carry the emotional weight

**Key principle:** slides are *support* for the spoken message. The founder is the spotlight. If the slides are so dense the judge is reading them, the founder loses the spotlight.

---

## 5. Nothing memorable

**The failure:** the pitch is competent, correct, and completely forgettable. Ten minutes after the judge closes the tab, they can't remember what the product does. In a funnel of 1,500+ submissions, forgettable = dead.

**How to check:** imagine the judge pitching *your* pitch to another judge in the hallway 30 minutes later. What's the one sentence they'd use? If you can't answer that cleanly, the pitch has no hook.

**Fixes (pick one or two, don't try to do all):**
- **One quotable line** embedded in the pitch that captures the idea. ("Buy anything with stablecoins." "Kickstarter that actually pays you.")
- **One wow-moment stat.** A number so surprising the judge remembers it. ("10× industry-average retention." "$435k revenue, never a down week.")
- **One vivid visual.** A single image that captures the pain or the product so well the judge recalls it. (Nomu's "desperate guy on the sofa". Surf Cache's QR-code-at-food-stall image.)
- **One unexpected founder insight.** "We realized that 80% of nomads carry two wallets and three cards. We replaced all of them." That kind of line sticks.

**Test:** after drafting, ask yourself — if the judge only remembers *one thing* from this pitch 24 hours later, what do I want it to be? Then make sure that one thing is unmissable in the script.

---

## Running the self-check

Before showing the user a draft in build mode, or before returning review feedback, go through these 5 patterns and answer each with **pass / fail / risk**.

If 3+ are fail/risk, the draft isn't ready — iterate before showing the user. If 1–2 are fail, flag them explicitly to the user along with the fix.

```
1. Strong hook in first 15 seconds? [ pass / fail / risk ]
2. Trust (concrete facts, no conditional tense)? [ pass / fail / risk ]
3. Problem sold with data + consequence? [ pass / fail / risk ]
4. Slides clean (≤4-word titles, one message each, brand identity)? [ pass / fail / risk ]
5. Something memorable embedded? [ pass / fail / risk ]
```
