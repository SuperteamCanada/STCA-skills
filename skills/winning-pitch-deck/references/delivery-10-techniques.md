# The 10 delivery techniques

The deck is maybe 50% of the pitch. The other 50% is delivery — *how* the founder says what they say. A great deck with monotone delivery loses; a decent deck with sharp delivery wins.

These techniques come directly from Sofian's framework. Walk through them with the founder at the end of build mode, and always cross-check in review mode.

---

## 1. Pitch like it's the first time

The founder has said these words a hundred times. The judge hasn't heard them once. If the founder rushes, sounds bored, or says "obviously" and "of course", they kill the surprise — and surprise is how attention is earned.

**What to do:** imagine the judge knows nothing about the space. Introduce yourself fully. Don't compress. "Hi, I'm [name], founder of [product], we do [one-liner]" — then *pause* before jumping to the problem.

**Ban words:** "obviously", "of course", "as you know", "as we all know". These signal "this is boring for me" and the audience mirrors that feeling.

**Emotion matches the beat.**
- Problem slide → let it affect you; tone drops
- Solution + value prop → energy up, smile
- Traction → serious, confident, this is what we got
- Close → clear, inviting, leave them wanting the next step

---

## 2. Pitch a teaser, not a full movie

The goal is not to transmit every fact about the product in 3 minutes. The goal is to make the judge *want to dig in further* — watch the demo video, read the weekly report, book a call.

**What to do:** cut anything that doesn't earn the next click. If a detail doesn't make the judge more curious, delete it. Leave gaps the judge fills in by clicking the CTA.

**Test:** after the 3 minutes, what does the judge *still want to know*? If the answer is "nothing", the pitch was a full movie. Rewrite.

---

## 3. Know who's listening

Colosseum judges aren't just hackathon organizers — **they're a VC**. Their investment thesis is: fund startups that can raise multiple rounds and reach high valuations. Same thesis as any top-tier fund.

The judges are also exhausted. They're watching hundreds of pitches back-to-back, competing with their own phones and email. If the pitch doesn't fit "fundable, scalable startup" narrative, even a beautiful pitch loses.

**What to do:** build the pitch knowing the audience's bias. Emphasize scalability, market size, unit economics, defensibility. For live demo-day audiences, adjust — but for Colosseum, judge = VC.

**Specifically for the Colosseum interview round** (if the founder advances): the VC will challenge the business model, the moat ("why can't anyone copy you tomorrow?"), and the founder's seriousness ("is this a hackathon project or a 5-year company?"). The founder should start preparing interview answers *while building the pitch*, not after.

---

## 4. Surprise starts with the problem

If the space is crowded (another DeFi protocol, another AI agent, another wallet), the founder's differentiation doesn't come from the solution — many products can solve the same problem. It comes from how they *frame* the problem in a way the judge hasn't heard before.

**What to do:** share **founder insight**. "We studied the problem, tried X, realized Y, which is why we built Z." Walk the judge through the exploration, not just the conclusion. The insight is what makes the problem feel new.

**Techniques to surprise on the problem:**
- A stat the judge hasn't seen before (*"67% of Kickstarter users never return"*)
- A recent event in the ecosystem (news headline, viral tweet, protocol hack)
- A user story with emotional texture ("I was in Bangkok trying to buy pad thai and couldn't because…")
- A contradiction ("demand for X is growing 200%, but 80% of attempts fail because…")

**Start with what hurts.** Lost money, lost time, missed opportunity — those are the three real pains. Frame the problem as one of those three, concretely.

---

## 5. If niche, make it relatable with a comparison

For a niche or technically complex product, the judge may not have context. Don't try to educate fully — that burns time. Use a comparison.

**Pattern:** *"[Known product] did this for [Known market]; we're doing it for [Your market]."*
- "Kickstarter for crypto products" → Nomu
- "Pokémon Go for crypto" → Loot Go
- "Amazon checkout but with stablecoins" → Spend

One comparison and the judge has a mental model. Now the pitch can build on it.

---

## 6. Write every transition between slides

The pitch is one continuous narrative funnel, not a slide deck read aloud. Every slide-to-slide transition needs a scripted sentence.

**Why this matters:** transitions control rhythm, kill monotony, and signal "this is a story, not a list". A pitch with scripted transitions *feels* coherent even if the individual slides are simple. A pitch without them feels like a student reading a book report.

**Examples:**
- Problem → Value Prop: *"And that's exactly what we're solving."*
- Value Prop → Features: *"Here's how it works."*
- Business Model → Traction: *"And we're already doing it."*
- Traction → Roadmap: *"Here's what ships next."*

See `slide-by-slide.md` for a full list. Then **rewrite them in the founder's voice.** The point isn't the exact words — it's that the transition is intentional.

---

## 7. Control what the judge sees (use animations)

If a slide has three items and all three appear at once, the judge reads ahead and stops listening to the founder. Every list should be revealed one item at a time.

**Where to animate:**
- Feature bullets (reveal each as you say it)
- Roadmap milestones (reveal each quarter as you get there)
- Traction metrics (reveal each one — builds suspense)

**Rule:** the judge's eye should follow the founder's voice, not race ahead.

---

## 8. Ban conditional forms

Tense discipline is one of the highest-leverage rules in the whole framework. It takes one pass to fix and doubles trust instantly.

**Ban these words:**
- "trying to" → *"We're trying to solve payment friction"* → **"We solve payment friction"**
- "hope to" → *"We hope to reach 10k users"* → **"We reach 10k users by Q3"**
- "aim to" → *"We aim to build the best wallet"* → **"We're building the best wallet"**
- "would" / "should" / "could" → all forbidden
- "what we're trying to do is…" → **"what we do is…"**

**The rule:** present tense for what exists, past tense for what shipped, future tense **only** on the roadmap slide.

**Why this matters:** "trying to" communicates that the founder is *attempting*, not *doing*. A VC hears that and files the pitch under "maybe". Decisive tense says "this exists, it works, we're shipping it".

This is the single easiest 10x improvement in most founder pitches.

---

## 9. Record yourself, sit 24 hours, rehearse 50+ times

Sofian's co-founder rehearsed their winning pitch **50+ times**. They locked themselves in an Airbnb in Vietnam for 3 days. This is not optional if the founder wants to win.

**The rehearsal protocol:**

1. **Record** (Loom, phone selfie, whatever) once you have a draft pitch.
2. **Wait 24 hours.** Watching immediately is too harsh — the founder will hate everything and abandon good material.
3. **Audio-only pass.** Listen without video. Note: filler words? Monotone? Conditional verbs? Same energy on every slide?
4. **Video-only pass.** Watch with sound off. Note: using hands? Smiling on the right beats? Serious on the right beats? Or flat the whole way?
5. **Both together.** Fix. Re-record.
6. **Repeat 50 times.** Time yourself every run. Rehearse the *transitions* as much as the slides — they're where most founders fumble.
7. **Share with mentors, friends, fellow founders.** Get external feedback. Pitch in public if possible. It's really hard to self-assess without an outside read.

**The goal:** the script becomes second nature. The founder isn't thinking about words anymore — they're thinking about the judge's face. That's when the pitch lands.

**Analogy:** learning an instrument. First you focus on the fingering. Once it's automatic, you can play with feel. Same with the pitch.

## 10. Stage rules (for live or recorded pitch)

Basic production hygiene. Many founders skip this, so doing it alone puts the founder in the top 20%.

**For recorded pitch video:**
- **$30 mic.** Triples audio quality vs laptop mic. Non-negotiable.
- **Good lighting.** Face the window, not away from it.
- **Clean background.** Blank wall or intentional branded setup. Not a messy bedroom.
- **Full-screen deck.** When screen-sharing, hide browser tabs, notifications, dock.
- **Camera at eye level.** Not looking down at a laptop.
- **One take or clean cuts.** If the founder is on camera next to the deck, make sure the layout works.

**For live stage pitch:**
- **Look above the audience's eyes** if watching reactions would rattle you. They can't tell the difference.
- **Own the stage.** Walk with intention. Don't pace.
- **Pause at transitions.** Silence is the most underused tool in pitching.
- **Enjoy it.** Created the stage, now enjoy standing on it.

## Checklist to deliver at the end of build mode

Paste this into the final response, customized to the founder's specific deck:

```
[ ] Hook in first 15 seconds (or exceptional traction/authority opener)
[ ] No "obviously" / "of course" / "as you know" anywhere
[ ] Tense check: no "trying to" / "hope to" / "aim to" / "would" / "should"
[ ] Future tense only on roadmap slide
[ ] Every slide-to-slide transition has a scripted sentence
[ ] Demo appears before 2:00
[ ] Demo is 20–30 seconds, no music, one flow
[ ] Traction slide has 3 real metrics (no X followers)
[ ] Business model slide exists
[ ] Team slide leads with one outstanding achievement
[ ] Ends with a CTA, not "thank you"
[ ] One memorable / quotable line embedded somewhere
[ ] Rehearsed with the 24-hour audio-then-video protocol
[ ] Timed at ≤ 3:00 (target: 2:30–2:45 so there's breathing room)
[ ] $30 mic, clean background, good lighting if recording
[ ] Legends.fund / product-page QR code on close slide (if Colosseum Frontier)
```
