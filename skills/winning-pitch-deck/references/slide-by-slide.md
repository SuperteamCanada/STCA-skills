# Slide-by-slide guidance

Winning examples are drawn from Colosseum hackathon winners Sofian coached or analyzed: **Surf Cache** (neo-bank for digital nomads), **Nomu** (pre-order crypto commerce, pays backers when products sell), **Spend** (stablecoin purchases on Amazon/eBay), **CFL / Crypto Fantasy League**, **Loot Go** (Pokémon Go-style crypto mobile game).

For each slide below: **purpose → what to put on it → what to avoid → example.** Not every slide is required — optional ones are marked.

---

## 1. Intro / Hook slide

**Purpose.** First touchpoint. Judge is meeting you for the first time, hook attention in the first 15 seconds.

**On-slide:**
- Product logo (which is usually the product name)
- One-liner — *what you do*, in words a judge can repeat
- *Optional:* founder photo + name, deck title ("Frontier Colosseum submission")

**Avoid:**
- Rushing past this slide to "save time" — half of weak pitches do this. Relax. "Hi, my name is X, founder of Y, we do Z." Then problem slide.
- Visionary-transformation-of-the-industry language. "Revolutionizing how humans exchange value" is noise.
- Clutter. If the judge can't recite the one-liner back to another judge in the hallway, rewrite it.

**Winning example (Surf Cache):** logo + "the neo-bank for people who move" + one phone mockup. Everyone knows what a neo-bank is. "People who move" targets the user profile (nomads). Total elements on the slide: 3.

**Winning example (Nomu):** "Kickstarter that actually pays you." Uses a comparison to something the judge already knows (Kickstarter) and points at the differentiator (pays backers) in the same breath. Technique: if you're doing a known Web2 thing in Web3, use the "X for Y" comparison as your one-liner.

**Hook technique.** If you have exceptional traction or founder credentials, open with *that* before the problem. Authority first earns attention for the rest of the pitch. Otherwise, go straight to the problem.

## 2. Problem slide

**Purpose.** Make the judge feel the pain. This is the most important emotional beat in the deck. "If you sell me the problem, you don't need to sell me the solution."

**On-slide:**
- **One** main problem, not a list of three or four
- Consequences if unsolved (lost money, lost time, missed opportunity — those are the only three pains that really land)
- Data that backs the pain (a stat, a tweet screenshot, a news headline — something that says *this is happening now*)
- An image that makes the pain visceral (Nomu used a desperate-looking guy on a sofa)

**Avoid:**
- A bullet list of problems. Pick one.
- "Obviously" / "of course". Kills the surprise.
- Over-explaining an obvious problem (vibe trading, ICO crashes, token dumps — everyone knows). Under-explaining a niche problem (local QR payment systems in SE Asia — judge from North America doesn't know).
- Conditional language ("we're trying to solve…"). See delivery-10-techniques.md rule 8.

**Winning example (Nomu):** "67% of Kickstarter users never return." → then explains *why*: you wait months, all you get is the product, no upside. The stat earns trust; the explanation earns empathy.

**Winning example (Spend):** "Neo-banks and crypto debit cards are popping up [trend] — but they're limited by geo, KYC is required, and crypto cards are bad in many countries [pain]." The structure is *demand exists → demand is blocked* — the judge can feel the transition coming.

**Winning example (Surf Cache):** Emerging-markets payment. They educate with an image of someone scanning a Vietnamese QR code at a food stall. A North American judge didn't know you can't pay cash for street food in Vietnam — now they do, and they feel the problem.

**Founder-insight technique.** "We studied the problem, tried X, realized Y, and that's why we built Z." Judges love hearing the exploration, not just the conclusion. This is how you make the problem feel new even if the space is crowded.

**If the problem is niche:** use a smart comparison. "This exists for [well-known thing]; we're doing it for [your thing]." Makes it relatable in one sentence.

## 3. Value Proposition (transition slide)

**Purpose.** Bridge between problem and product. One sentence that tells the user what they *get*.

**On-slide:**
- One sentence, direct-to-user, present tense
- Large type, often no image needed — this is a pause slide

**Avoid:**
- Feature-speak ("leveraging on-chain attestations for…"). Benefit-speak ("buy anything with stablecoins"). Big difference.
- Putting this before the problem. The problem slide earns the right to say the value prop.

**Winning examples:**
- Nomu: *"Pre-order products and earn when they sell."*
- Spend: *"Buy anything with stablecoins."*
- Surf Cache: *"Pay locally. Your money works. Perks when you travel."*

Test: read your value prop to someone who doesn't know your product. If they can repeat it back, you've got it. If not, rewrite.

**Transition sentence** (say as you click to this slide): *"This is why we built [product]."* or *"Here's what we do about it."*

## 4. Features / How it works

**Purpose.** Give the judge just enough context to watch the demo without getting lost.

**On-slide:**
- 3 core features or a 3-step user journey ("Browse → Add to Cart → Get Paid")
- Each feature framed as a user *benefit*, not a technical capability

**Avoid:**
- A feature matrix. This isn't a product page.
- Jargon. "Account abstraction" is a feature; "you don't need a seed phrase" is a benefit.
- More than 3 items — use animations to reveal one at a time if needed.

**Winning example (Surf Cache):** "**Pay locally** (use anywhere) · **Your money works** (earn yield) · **Perks as a traveler**." Each is a feature but phrased as a user benefit.

## 5. Demo

**Purpose.** Show the product actually working. This is the credibility moment.

**On-slide:**
- A screen recording or phone mockup of **one** main feature, end to end
- Caption showing the "in-situation" use case ("Scanning QR at a Bangkok street stall")

**Avoid:**
- Background music. Creates a pitch-inside-the-pitch and the judge tunes out when it ends.
- Fancy animations. Simplicity wins.
- Speed-runs covering every feature. You'll lose the judge. Pick one flow.
- Demo appearing at 2:10 or later in a 3-min pitch. Judge has already left.

**Duration:** 20–30 seconds. Not more.

**Position:** After features, before market. Never at the end.

**Speaker script tip:** "Let me show you. [click]. Download the app. Connect wallet. Scan. Paid. That's it." Calm, no rush, let the video carry the demo.

## 6. Market

**Purpose.** Show that this is a real, big, capturable market — not a science project.

**On-slide:**
- User categories (not TAM/SAM/SOM triangle — that's for investor decks, not hackathon pitches)
- Intersection / target user profile with a number attached

**Avoid:**
- The boring "TAM $50B, SAM $5B, SOM $500M" slide. Judges glaze over.
- Round numbers with no source ("there are billions of crypto users").

**Winning example (CFL):** Two numbers side by side — "2.5M crypto traders · 62.5M fantasy sports users" — and the product sits in the intersection = 2.5M addressable users. Steal the visual.

**Winning example (Surf Cache):** three-circle Venn: Digital Nomads ∩ Local Crypto Earners ∩ Travelers Who Use Crypto = ~75M potential users. The intersection *is* the slide.

---

## 7. Trends (optional)

**Purpose.** Prove the market is growing, not shrinking.

**On-slide:**
- 1–3 growth percentages with sources or years attached
- Tight. One slide, three stats max.

**Winning example (Surf Cache):** "+153% growth in digital nomads · +179% growth in freelancers paid in crypto · +200% stablecoin growth in 2025." Three lines, done.

**Skip this slide if:** the trends aren't strong or you don't have solid sources. Weak trends hurt you more than no trends.

---

## 8. Competition (optional)

**Purpose.** Address the question the judge is already asking: "how are you different from [obvious competitor]?"

**On-slide:**
- 2–3 competitors max
- A simple comparison matrix — features or positioning
- Do *not* try to win on every dimension. Pick one or two where you're clearly superior.

**Speaker tip:** Don't walk through every cell of the matrix. Point at the one dimension where you win. "Unlike Cast, we work in countries where their cards don't — that's our wedge."

**Skip this slide if:** there's no obvious competitor the judge would think of. Don't invent competitors to make yourself look thorough.

---

## 9. Business Model

**Purpose.** Show the value-capture mechanism. **This slide is non-negotiable.** Skipping it says "I'm pitching a concept."

**On-slide:**
- How you make money, in one line or diagram
- Transaction fee % (most common for crypto products), subscription, marketplace take rate, spread, etc.

**Avoid:**
- 5-year ARR projections for a hackathon pitch. Those belong in an investor deck. The business model slide is about the *mechanism*, not the forecast.
- Vague "we'll figure out monetization later." Instant fail signal.

**Winning pattern:** "2% transaction fee on every purchase." One line. Done.

## 10. Traction

**Purpose.** Prove you can actually execute, not just pitch. "If your traction is great, whatever else you say, you win."

**On-slide:**
- **3 metrics.** The best ones:
  1. **Revenue** (any amount — $1 of real revenue beats 10,000 waitlist sign-ups)
  2. **Growth rate** (week-over-week or month-over-month, trending up)
  3. **Usage** (active users, transactions, matches played, GMV — a real product metric, not a vanity metric)

**Avoid:**
- **X/Twitter followers.** VCs and Colosseum judges read this as a red flag. It signals you're optimizing for optics, not product. Cut it.
- Cumulative totals that hide a flat curve. Show growth *rate*, not just totals.
- Made-up projections labeled as "expected traction." Judges can smell this.

**Winning example (Nomu):** "$435k revenue, growing weekly, never a down week." The "never a down week" is the important part — the judge feels "this grows with or without me."

**Extrapolation technique.** If your numbers are small in absolute terms but strong relative to industry averages, say so. Loot Go: *"50% D7 retention — 10× the mobile game average."* The comparison turns a small number into a big signal.

**Position:** right after business model. But if your traction is exceptional, open with it *before* the problem to establish authority.

## 11. Partners / Awards (optional)

**Purpose.** Third-party validation.

**On-slide:**
- Logos **with one-line explanations** of what the partnership actually does
- Awards, grants, accelerator acceptances, notable advisors

**Avoid:**
- A wall of logos with no context. "We did co-marketing" means nothing. "We integrated their SDK and they route 12% of their users to our app" means something.
- Padding the slide with logos of companies you had a single call with.

**Advisor technique (for solo founders):** land 2–3 top mentors in the field as named advisors. Gives social proof that people at the top of the industry are betting on you.

## 12. Growth Strategy

**Purpose.** Show the engine that takes you from current traction to the next milestone.

**On-slide:**
- A target (10k users, 100k users, $100k MRR)
- 3 solid distribution channels. Not 6 vague ones.

**Avoid:**
- "Social media + SEO + influencers + partnerships + community + PR." That's not a plan, that's a wish list.
- Channels you haven't actually tried yet. If you've never shipped one viral loop, don't claim "viral growth" is channel #1.

**Winning pattern:** three channels, each with a concrete plan. "Super-apps partnership for SE Asia distribution · Nomad-community content partnerships · App Store ASO around 'crypto travel card' keywords."

---

## 13. Roadmap

**Purpose.** Show what ships next and when.

**On-slide:**
- Dated milestones: Q2, Q3, Q4 (not "Phase 1 / Phase 2 / Phase 3" — dates feel real, phases feel hypothetical)
- **One big thing per quarter**, not a laundry list

**This is the only slide where future tense is allowed.** Everywhere else: present or past.

**Avoid:**
- A 30-item roadmap crammed into one slide. Becomes unreadable.
- Vague milestones ("scale the product"). Use concrete: "launch on Seeker", "expand to Thailand", "partnership with X".

**Winning pattern:** "Q2: Launch on Seeker · Q3: Expand to Thailand + Vietnam · Q4: Open B2B API."

---

## 14. Team

**Purpose.** Prove you're the team to ship this. **Not** a bio page.

**On-slide:**
- **One outstanding achievement** the team (or key member) has already done that's directly relevant
- Faces + names + roles as secondary info, not the main message

**Avoid:**
- "CEO — 10 years in marketing. CTO — ex-Google. Head of BD — ex-Solana Foundation." That's a boring team slide and Sofian will call it out. It's the default and it costs you the slide.
- Listing every team member's full bio.

**Winning example (CFL):** huge banner — "We built [previous game], reached 320k users." The message is: *we know how to build games and acquire users; we're doing it again.* Individual bios are tiny beneath.

**Winning example (Loot Go):** landed an advisor from the Pokémon Go team. The slide highlights the advisor, not the founders' bios. Top-of-industry advisor = top-of-industry signal.

**Solo-founder technique:** land 2–3 named advisors from the top of your field. That *is* your team slide.

---

## 15. Conclusion + CTA

**Purpose.** Leave the judge with (a) one memorable line and (b) something concrete to do next.

**On-slide:**
- One-line reminder of what you do (echo the hook)
- One-line vision (optional, keep it tight)
- **A call to action.** This is the part 90% of founders miss.

**CTA options:**
- "Watch the longer demo → [link / QR]"
- "Book a 15-min call with me → [link]"
- "Try the product → [link]"
- **Legends.fund trick:** QR code to the product page. Judges review weeks later and see *current* traction, proving the team is still building.

**Avoid:**
- "Thank you." Not a CTA. Costs you the post-pitch attention.
- Generic "contact us" email. Give one specific, frictionless action.

**Speaker script:** last 10 seconds. "We're [one-liner]. [One memorable line.] Scan to see where we're at today — we ship every week." [pause.]

## Transition sentences (glue between slides)

Every transition needs a scripted sentence. Examples:

- Intro → Problem: *"So let me tell you what we're seeing."*
- Problem → Value Prop: *"And that's exactly what we're solving."*
- Value Prop → Features: *"Here's how it works."*
- Features → Demo: *"Let me show you."*
- Demo → Market: *"And this isn't a small market."*
- Market → Business Model: *"So how do we actually make money?"*
- Business Model → Traction: *"And we're already doing it."*
- Traction → Growth: *"Here's how we scale this."*
- Growth → Roadmap: *"And here's what ships next."*
- Roadmap → Team: *"We're the team to do it."*
- Team → CTA: *"One more thing before we go."*

Rewrite these in the founder's voice. The point isn't the exact words — it's that the transition is *scripted*, not ad-libbed as "ok next slide".
